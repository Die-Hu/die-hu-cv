Background & Story

Growing up in rural Guizhou, China, I was first appointed class leader at age six — not because anyone asked if I wanted to, but because someone had to. That sense of stewardship has shaped everything since. When I volunteered for typhoon cleanup at my university in Xiamen and stood among uprooted trees and damaged streets for the first time, I felt the raw tension between nature's force and the fragile city we call home. That moment became a compass.

My research journey has taken me from tourism geography to ecological network construction, from land-use spatial planning across China's provinces to analyzing human mobility patterns with federal survey data in California. What looks like a winding path is, in fact, a deepening question: how does spatial structure shape outcomes — for people, for ecosystems, for the communities we build?

"I don't want human beings to pollute beautiful garden cities. I don't want haze to cover the blue sky! I am on the right track to protect mother earth!"

— Personal Statement

What Fuels Me

Read My Story

I was six years old and already class leader in a small village school in Guizhou. I did not fully understand what that meant at the time, but I understood the feeling — being the person who makes sure no one gets left out, who notices the quiet kid, who holds things together so everyone can do their part. That instinct has never left me. It followed me through seven summers of fieldwork across rural China, through a typhoon cleanup in Xiamen where strangers became a team in hours, and through a twenty-person land survey crew I coordinated in Hubei, where the best insight of the entire project came from a surveyor I almost did not think to ask. The pattern is always the same: the work gets better when everyone in the room feels like they belong there.

I have studied at five institutions across two countries, and the thing that has shaped me most at each one was never the curriculum — it was whether I found people I could grow alongside. A good team changes everything. It changes the questions you think to ask, the mistakes you are willing to make, and the kind of work you are brave enough to try. I care deeply about the research problems I work on — mobility, accessibility, who gets included in the design of cities — but I care about them because they are fundamentally about people being seen. And I have learned that you cannot do justice to that kind of question from inside a silo. It requires people who challenge you, support you, and hold you accountable to the communities the work is meant to serve.

What I want to build going forward is not just a body of research. It is an environment — a lab culture, a collaboration, a room — where people from different backgrounds bring their full perspective and feel safe doing it. Where junior members are heard, where fieldwork is a conversation and not just data collection, and where the human stakes of the work stay visible in every meeting, every model, every result. That is the kind of place where I do my best thinking. I want to help create it for others, too.

GeoAI
Featured · AAG 2026

How We Move, What We Reach: Capabilities, Constraints & Daily Activity Space

GeoTrans Lab, UCSB · 2025–Present

Using 2017 NHTS California data (N=28,404), I developed a dwell-time-weighted Activity Space Indicator measuring how far people actually commit their daily time. Through Spatial Error Models and Geographically Weighted Regression, the work reveals that individual capabilities (worker status, driver license, vehicle access) dominate activity space extent, while the effect of accessibility is spatially variable — compressing travel in dense urban cores but having little effect in rural areas.
